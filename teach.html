The T.E.A.C.H. Framework

An easy-to-remember acronym for teachers to master effective AI prompting. Each letter represents a key step in crafting a strong prompt.

T - Task

Essential

Start with an action verb that clearly defines what you want the AI to do.

Examples:

  • Generate a list of...
  • Write a lesson plan about...
  • Create an assessment for...
  • Explain the concept of...
  • Analyze this student writing sample...

Why it matters: The task sets the primary direction for the AI. Without a clear task, the AI might generate unfocused or inappropriate content.

E - Example

Important

Provide models, frameworks, or samples to guide the AI toward your desired output style and quality.

Examples:

  • Format it like this: [example format]
  • Use this writing style: [example paragraph]
  • Follow this structure: [outline or template]
  • Include sections similar to: [example sections]

Why it matters: Examples dramatically improve the AI's understanding of your expectations and help it match your vision more precisely.

A - Audience

Helpful

Define the persona or role the AI should take on and who the content is for.

Examples:

  • Write as if you are a 3rd grade teacher explaining...
  • Create content appropriate for middle school students...
  • Design this for English language learners who are...
  • Write this for parents of kindergarteners...

Why it matters: Defining the audience helps the AI adjust its language, complexity, and tone to be appropriate for the intended readers.

C - Context

Important

Give background details to refine the response and make it more relevant.

Examples:

  • We're studying the water cycle in our unit about...
  • My students struggle with multiplication facts, especially...
  • Our school follows the XYZ reading curriculum...
  • This is for a classroom with limited technology access...

Why it matters: Context helps the AI tailor its response to your specific situation, making the output more useful and relevant to your needs.

H - How to Format

Helpful

Specify the structure and organization you prefer for the response.

Examples:

  • Format as a table with these columns: [column names]
  • Present this as bullet points under clear headings
  • Organize this in chronological order
  • Create a step-by-step guide with numbered steps
  • Include a materials list at the beginning
  • Structure this as an "I do, we do, you do" lesson
  • Format this as a two-column table with pros and cons
  • Present this as a rubric with 3 performance levels

Why it matters: Formatting specifications ensure the AI's response is organized in a way that's easy to use and suits your intended purpose.

Putting It All Together

Example Complete Prompt:

T (Task): Generate 5 math word problems
E (Example): Like this example: "Sarah has 24 cookies and wants to share them equally among 6 friends. How many cookies will each friend receive?"
A (Audience): For 3rd grade students who are just learning division
C (Context): We've been working on division with remainders, and many students are struggling with interpreting what to do with the remainder in real-world contexts
H (How to Format): Present each problem followed by a "Teacher Note" explaining how to guide students who get stuck

What Makes This Effective:

  • Clear task that specifies exactly what's needed (5 math word problems)
  • Specific example that shows the level of complexity expected
  • Well-defined audience (3rd graders learning division)
  • Helpful context about current learning challenges
  • Practical formatting request that supports teaching needs

Why T.E.A.C.H.?

  • It's memorable for educators who are new to working with AI.
  • It reinforces teaching best practicesâ€”clear instructions, modeling, and structure.
  • It makes AI a more effective tool in the classroom by ensuring precise, engaging responses.

Component Importance Ranking

Component Importance Why
Task (T) Essential Without a clear task, the AI lacks direction and may produce irrelevant content.
Context (C) Important Context significantly improves relevance and helps the AI meet your specific needs.
Example (E) Important Examples dramatically improve output quality by showing exactly what you want.
Audience (A) Helpful Helps adjust language complexity and tone but can be inferred from context in some cases.
How to Format (H) Helpful Formatting enhances usability but many responses can be effective in multiple formats.

Prompt Evolution: From Basic to Advanced

Watch how a teacher's prompt evolves from basic to advanced as each element of the T.E.A.C.H. framework is applied. This demonstrates the dramatic improvement in AI responses when using a structured approach to prompting.

Prompt Evolution

Scenario: A 4th grade teacher wants to create a science activity about the water cycle

Level 1: Basic Prompt (T only)

T: Create a water cycle activity.

Likely AI Response:

A generic water cycle activity that might not match the teacher's grade level, available materials, or curriculum alignment needs. The teacher would need to heavily modify the response.

Level 2: Better Prompt (T + C)

T: Create a water cycle activity.
C: This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity.

Likely AI Response:

A more appropriate activity for 4th graders that considers time and material constraints. However, it might not include clear instructions or align with the teacher's teaching style.

Level 3: Strong Prompt (T + C + A)

T: Create a water cycle activity.
C: This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity.
A: My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work.

Likely AI Response:

A collaborative hands-on activity appropriate for 4th graders with consideration for time and material constraints. The teaching approach will match the students' learning preferences, but the exact format might not be what the teacher had in mind.

Level 4: Excellent Prompt (T + C + A + H)

T: Create a water cycle activity.
C: This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity.
A: My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work.
H: Structure this as a bullet-pointed list with sections for objective, materials, step-by-step instructions, and assessment questions.

Likely AI Response:

A well-structured, grade-appropriate collaborative activity with clear organization. The teacher can easily implement this activity with minimal modifications needed. However, it might still require creativity from the teacher to make it unique.

Level 5: Expert Prompt (All T.E.A.C.H. Elements)

T: Create a water cycle activity.
E: I did a similar activity last year where students created "mini terrariums" in plastic cups with lids to observe condensation and evaporation, but I want something new this year.
A: My students are hands-on learners who enjoy collaborative group work.
C: This is for 4th graders who have already learned the basic stages of the water cycle. We have limited materials and only 30 minutes for the activity. We're focusing on how the water cycle impacts local ecosystems.
H: Structure this as a bullet-pointed list with sections for objective, materials, step-by-step instructions, and assessment questions.

Likely AI Response:

A tailored, innovative activity that builds on prior experience, meets the teacher's specific pedagogical goals, addresses curriculum connections to ecosystems, and is presented in a ready-to-use format. The prompt's completeness allows the AI to generate a highly effective, creative, and practical response that aligns precisely with the teacher's needs.

What This Demonstrates:

  • Even a basic prompt (Task only) can get a response, but it's likely to be generic and require significant adaptation.
  • Adding Context significantly improves relevance to your specific situation.
  • Including Audience helps tailor the response to your students' needs and preferences.
  • Specifying How to Format makes the response immediately more usable.
  • Providing an Example dramatically improves the AI's understanding of your expectations and helps it generate more innovative alternatives.

When to Use All Elements

  • For complex requests where precision matters
  • When creating curriculum materials you'll use with students
  • For assessment design or rubric creation
  • When you need a high-quality resource with minimal editing
  • For specialized or technical content

When a Simpler Prompt Is Fine

  • For quick informational questions (just Task + Context)
  • When brainstorming initial ideas (Task + minimal Context)
  • For personal use materials that you'll heavily modify
  • When exploring possibilities before refining
  • For straightforward factual responses

A Teacher's Internal Dialogue

Here's what a teacher might think while crafting a prompt using the T.E.A.C.H. framework:

Task: "I need to create a fraction unit assessment. So I'll start with 'Create a math assessment on fractions...' Wait, that's too vague. Let me be more specific: 'Design a 10-question assessment to evaluate understanding of equivalent fractions and fraction operations.'"

Example: "What kind of problems do I want? I should show the AI an example so it understands the level... 'Include questions similar to: Find the equivalent fraction for 3/4 with a denominator of 12.'"

Audience: "These are 5th graders, so not too easy but not too challenging either. 'This is for 5th grade students who have been working with fractions for about three weeks. Several students still confuse addition and multiplication of fractions.'"

Context: "What else would help the AI create the right assessment? 'This assessment should align with Common Core standard 5.NF.A.1 and will be used as a summative assessment before moving to decimals. I want to identify students who need additional support before moving on.'"

How to Format: "How do I want this organized? 'Format this as a printable assessment with a mix of multiple choice (4 questions), short answer (3 questions), and word problems (3 questions). Include an answer key and scoring guide showing which questions align with which skills.'"
